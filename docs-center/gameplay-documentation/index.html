Player guide

Gameplay Documentation

Collatz Concourse is a competitive number-race game where pulse movement reveals prime factors, captured primes build Harmony, and shared factors create combat.

Turn Procedure

  1. Start the active player's turn.
  2. Roll 1d6 and gain that much resonance.
  3. Resolve the mandatory Collatz pulse step: even pulses halve, odd pulses become 3N + 1.
  4. Capture one prime value from the new pulse if one is available.
  5. Use optional actions: forced jump, pulse modification, Prime Division, normal attack, or Prime Theft.
  6. End the turn, apply resonance retention, check victory, and pass to the next player.

AI Pilots

The signed-in account occupies the main cockpit. Local rival seats use AI pilots that favor new distinct primes, valuable captures, favorable combat, Prime Theft, and resonance-efficient pulse growth. Select a rival craft on the Race Vector to load that competitor into the target/query screen and focus attack controls on them.

The current heuristic plans divide-by-2 jump loops, growth-oriented odd turn endings, deliberate even rebuild endings, and distinct-prime versus Harmony phases. Difficulty profiles and teaching variants are planned but are not yet selectable.

Resources

  • Pulse: The player's current positive integer position.
  • Captured Primes: A multiset of captured prime factors. Duplicates count for Harmony.
  • Resonance: Spendable energy for forced jumps, pulse modifications, and attacks.
  • Plasma Turbulence: Persistent instability from repeating the same prime during one turn; its binary log increases every optional-action cost.
  • Harmony: Sum of all captured primes, including duplicates.

Resonance Costs

  • A forced 3N + 1 jump costs floor(log2(pulse)) resonance, calculated before the jump.
  • A prime-fueled pulse modification costs floor(log2(prime)) resonance and discards that prime token.
  • Prime division sacrifices one captured prime no larger than the pulse, replaces the pulse with the integer quotient, and gains floor(log2(old pulse)) - floor(log2(prime)) + floor(log2(remainder)) resonance. Omit the remainder term when it is zero.
  • Each repeated use of a prime in one turn adds plasma turbulence equal to the number of its previous uses that turn. All optional actions add floor(log2(turbulence + 1)) to their resonance cost.
  • Plasma turbulence halves at the end of each player's turn, rounded down. A turn with no optional actions halves it a second time.
  • At end of turn, count all owned prime tokens, including duplicates. Resonance above floor(log2(token count)) decays by 50%, rounded down.

Combat

  1. An attack requires at least one shared prime value in attacker and defender pulses.
  2. The attacker chooses a shared initiating prime and pays resonance equal to that prime plus the current turbulence surcharge.
  3. Each prime value may initiate only one attack per turn.
  4. Assault Harmony Dice count attacker factor occurrences that also appear in the defender pulse.
  5. Shield Dissonance Dice count defender factor occurrences absent from the attacker pulse.
  6. Each die rerolls on 2 or 3, succeeds on 4 through 6, and fails on 1.
  7. The victor chooses a shared prime to divide out of the defeated pulse. Ties change no pulse.

Prime Theft

If pulses match exactly and both players have captured primes, the attacker may declare Prime Theft instead of a normal attack. The combat roll is normal, but the victor steals one captured prime from the loser. A tie steals nothing. Prime Theft always forces the attacker's turn to end.

Victory

A player wins at end of turn when they reach both selected targets. Distinct-prime climb targets are Foothill 10, Mountain 20, or Everest 40. Harmony distance targets are Sprint 1,000, Run 50,000, or Marathon 1,000,000.

The Race Vector display plots log2(1 + Harmony) distance horizontally and distinct-prime elevation vertically, keeping geometric Harmony growth readable while supporting a zero score. Crossing both green threshold lines moves a player craft into the upper-right victory sector. A translucent vector from each craft points to that player's pulse on the same logarithmic scale, making growth potential and approaching pulse matches easier to compare.

After victory, the winning pilot's recorded progress route remains visible on the Race Vector.
